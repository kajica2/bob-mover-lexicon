Engraving Rules for Jazz Transcription

When transcribing jazz audio for study or performance, the goal of engraving is clarity of intent — the notation must show exactly where each note sits relative to the beat, so that a reader can accurately reproduce the feel. Bob Mover's whole method is built on "meeting the beats" and understanding the difference between upbeats and downbeats (MC 10, 11, 16). If the notation hides the beat structure, the exercise fails.

Below are a set of engraving rules that will keep your transcriptions clean, readable, and rhythmically honest. They are tailored to the swing-based, mostly eighth-note vocabulary found in the JazzAbility Master Classes.

Rule 1 – The Swing Eighth-Note Convention

  • Notate all swinging eighth notes as straight eighths, never as dotted-eighth/sixteenth pairs (unless you are writing for a Latin or straight-8 feel).
  • Place the word "Swing" or the symbol two eighths = triplet quarter + eighth at the top of the chart.
  • This tells the player to interpret the eighths with a triplet subdivision, but it keeps the page uncluttered.
Correct:   ♪ ♪ ♪ ♪   (with "Swing" indication)

Incorrect: ♪.  ♪  ♪.  ♪   (for a swing tune)

Rule 2 – Use Explicit Triplets Only When They Break the Swing Pattern

  • Write a "3" triplet bracket only when the rhythm truly deviates from the swing-eighth norm — e.g., a group of three equal notes in the space of two normal eighth notes, or a quarter-note triplet over two beats.
  • For a line that is entirely triplet-based (e.g., a triple-time feel), write the whole section in 12/8 rather than cluttering 4/4 with endless "3" marks.
Swing eighths:   ♪ ♪ ♪ ♪   (no "3" needed)

Actual triplet:  ♪³   or   ♪³ ♪³ ♪³   (against the swing grid)

Rule 3 – Always Expose the Middle of the Bar (Beat 3)

  • In 4/4 time, never obscure beat 3 with a single note value that crosses it. If a note begins on the "and" of 2 and lasts into beat 3, break it into two tied notes that show beat 3 clearly.
  • The same applies to other strong beats — but beat 3 is the universal anchor in 4/4 swing.
Correct:    ♩.   _ tie _   ♪   (dotted quarter on beat 2½ tied to an eighth on beat 3)

Incorrect:  ♩.   ♪   without a tie (the dotted quarter crosses beat 3 invisibly)

This rule is essential for "Meet the Beats" exercises, where students must see exactly where "the and of 3" or "the and of 4" falls.

Rule 4 – Use Ties, Not Dots, for Syncopations That Cross Beats

  • Because swing eighths are uneven (roughly 2:1), dotted rhythms often create a false visual impression. Where a note starts on a weak subdivision and sustains into a strong beat, use a tie.
  • This keeps the underlying 8th-note grid visible, making it easier for the reader to feel the off-beat.
Swing feel phrase:   ♪  ♪  ♪‿♪  ♪

(The tie shows the note is held across the beat; a dotted eighth would hide the grid)

Rule 5 – Limit Your Rhythmic Palette to Quarters, Eighths, Triplets, and Half-Notes (Mostly)

  • For bebop and mainstream swing vocabulary, 90% of what you hear will fall into:
    • quarter notes
    • eighth notes (swung)
    • eighth-note triplets
    • quarter-note triplets (less frequent)
    • half notes and whole notes (for long tones)
  • Avoid 16th-notes unless the player is clearly double-timing (playing twice as fast). In that case, notate the double-time passage with 16ths, but mark "double time feel" above, and return to 8ths when the pace drops.
  • This guideline directly matches your request: only 8th notes, triplets, quarter notes, or similar. It keeps the transcription simple and rhythmically transparent.

Rule 6 – Articulation Marks Are Part of the Engraving

  • In Bob Mover's world, articulation defines the phrase — "pecking", "riffing", legato, accents. Use these symbols consistently:
    • Tenuto (—) : long, full value, slightly weighted.
    • Staccato (·) : short, detached.
    • Accent (>) : strong attack, often on offbeats.
    • Marcato (^) : heavy accent, usually on downbeats when the line demands it.
  • Without articulation, a perfect rhythmic transcription still sounds stiff. Listen to the recording and notate exactly how each note is touched.

Rule 7 – Phrase Marks and Slurs

  • Use slurs to group notes that are played legato (tongued only on the first note of the slur). Do not put a long slur over the entire line like a classical phrase marking — jazz musicians interpret that as a single breath, which may not be intended.
  • If the player uses breath accents or ghost notes, indicate them with parentheses or staccato marks.

Rule 8 – Tempo and "Pocket" Indications

  • Always write a metronome marking: e.g., ♩ = 120.
  • If the piece shifts to a deep pocket (half-time feel), you can either:
    • keep the same notation and write "half-time feel" above, or
    • temporarily re-notate with half-note beats, but that can be confusing. The first option is usually clearer.
  • Metronome placement: if the "deep pocket" is the primary feel, set the metronome to half notes and indicate that.

Example: Bird's "Yardbird Suite" Head — Bad vs. Good Engraving

Bad engraving

(obscures beats, uses dotted rhythms, no articulation)

♪.  ♪  ♪.  ♪   |   𝄽   𝄽   𝄽   𝄽

(no clue where beat 3 is, no swing indication)

Good engraving

(straight eighths, ties, articulations)

♩ ♪ ♪‿♪ ♪ ♪  |  ♩ ♪ ♪‿♪ ♪ ♪

(swing feel, tenuto on downbeats, staccato on upbeats)

With "Swing" and articulations written above.

Final Thought

Always remember Bob Mover's mantra: "Sing it first." The correct engraving will come naturally when you have internalised the line. The notation is merely a photograph of something that must already live in your ear and body. If the transcription doesn't make you tap your foot and feel the pocket, it's not yet correctly engraved.

Now apply these rules to the etudes and solos in the JazzAbility Master Classes — your transcriptions will be immediately more musical, and your students will thank you for showing them exactly where the upbeats and downbeats live.
