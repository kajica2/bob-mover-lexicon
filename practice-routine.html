The JazzAbility Daily Practice Routine

A 120-minute self-guided session rooted in Bob Mover's Master Classes.

How to Use This Routine

This is not a menu. It's a complete daily meal. Do it in order. The sequence is designed to move from the most fundamental skill (singing) through technique, ear training, rhythm, repertoire, creative application, and finally deep absorption. Each segment references specific Master Classes so you can return to the source videos for deeper study.

Equipment needed:

  • Your instrument
  • A metronome or metronome app (set to click on 2 and 4 for swing sections)
  • A playback device for recordings
  • A notebook for the tune-of-the-week analysis
  • A quiet space for mental practice
The non-negotiable rule: Sing everything before you play it.

0–10Singing: Scales, Intervals & Voice-Leading Lines MC 4, 12, 13, 15, 36–39

This wakes up your ear and connects your inner hearing to your voice. No instrument yet.

Minute 0–3: Scale Warm-Up

Sing the following scales on a neutral syllable ("da" or "la"), then on numerical solfeggio (1, 2, 3…), then on traditional solfeggio (do, re, mi…). Use a drone or a piano to check pitch.

  • Major scale – ascending and descending, one key per day (cycle through all twelve).
  • Harmonic minor – from the root.
  • Diminished scale (whole-half) – from the root.

Minute 3–6: Interval Patterns

Sing these patterns from the Master Classes. Each pattern is five notes, ascending and descending. Use numerical solfeggio.

Major scale patterns (from MC 35, 38):

1.  2 – 3 – 4 – ♯4 – 5   →   5 – ♭5 – 4 – 3 – 2

2.  2 – ♯2 – 3 – 4 – 5   →   5 – 4 – 3 – ♭3 – 2

3.  5 – ♯5 – 6 – 7 – 1   →   1 – 7 – 6 – ♭6 – 5

4.  5 – 6 – ♭7 – 7 – 1   →   1 – ♭7 – 6 – ♭6 – 5

Sing each pattern slowly, then increase tempo. Always hear the resolution to 1.

Minute 6–10: Voice-Leading Lines

Sing the four essential voice-leading movements through a ii–V–I. Use numerical solfeggio over the implied harmony. (From MC 20, 22, 36, 37.)

Pick one key per day. Sing a cappella, hearing the chords in your head.

Line 1:  9 (of ii)  →  ♯9 (of V)  →  Maj7 (of I)

Line 2:  7 (of ii)  →  ♭9 (of V)  →  5 (of I)

Line 3:  7 (of ii)  →  ♯11 (of V) →  9 (of I)

Line 4:  13 (of ii) →  ♭13 (of V) →  9 (of I)
Checkpoint: Can you sing all four lines without the instrument? If yes, move on.

10–25Technique: Permutations & Arpeggios Using the 8-Way Method MC 2, 35, 28

This is not mindless drill. Every technical exercise is ear training in disguise. Pick one exercise per day and work it through all twelve keys using the 8-Way Method.

The 8 Ways to Practise Just About Everything (MC 2)

For your chosen exercise, cycle through:

  1. Slow, metronome on every beat – accuracy first.
  2. Metronome on 2 and 4 – swing feel, even for straight eighths.
  3. Different octave – move the pattern up or down an octave.
  4. Sing it, then play it – always the golden rule.
  5. Start on a different beat – e.g., begin the phrase on the "and" of 1.
  6. Transpose by interval – move the pattern to the next key by ear, not by written chart.
  7. Invert it – if the pattern goes up, play it down; if it moves by thirds, move by sixths.
  8. Insert it into a tune – immediately apply the pattern to the tune of the week.

25–45Ear Training: Sing a Solo, Then Play It MC 18, 19, 21, 26, 27, 36, 37, 39

This is the heart of the JazzAbility method. You will learn jazz language the way children learn speech — by imitation, internalisation, and then reproduction.

The Process (20 minutes)

Phase 1 – Immersion (5 minutes):

  • Listen to the recording once without any analysis. Just absorb the sound, time, and feeling.
  • Tap your foot on 2 and 4, or try the deep pocket (half-time). (From MC 10, 11.)

Phase 2 – Sing the Melody (5 minutes):

  • Sing the head (the composed melody) along with the recording. If lyrics exist, learn them and sing with words.
  • Sing the melody a cappella, hearing the chord changes in your head.

Phase 3 – Sing the Solo (5 minutes):

  • Take one chorus of the solo. Sing it phrase by phrase with the recording. Use scat syllables or numerical solfeggio.
  • Repeat each phrase until it feels natural.
  • Bob Mover's instruction (MC 35): "We learn the line phrase by phrase by ear while the mind puts it together."

Phase 4 – Play the Solo (5 minutes):

  • Now pick up your instrument. Play the same chorus, phrase by phrase.
  • Match the articulation, dynamics, and time feel exactly. Use the transcription engraving rules: straight eighths, ties showing beat locations, articulation marks.
  • If a phrase is difficult, slow the recording down. Accuracy before speed.
Checkpoint: Can you sing and play one full chorus of the solo from memory? If not, stay with the same solo tomorrow.

45–60Rhythm: Meet-the-Beats Drill & Pocket Switching MC 5, 9, 10, 11, 12, 16, 17, 18, 19, 22

Rhythm is not a separate subject. It is the ground you walk on. This segment trains your body to feel the grid, the offbeats, and the deep pocket simultaneously.

Meet-the-Beats Drill (10 minutes)

Use the recording of Charlie Parker's "Yardbird Suite" or Bud Powell's "Hot House" (Massey Hall concert).

Step 1 – Identify the beats (2 minutes):

  • Listen and clap on every beat (1, 2, 3, 4). Get the tempo in your body.

Step 2 – Clap the offbeats (3 minutes):

  • Clap on the "and" of every beat. Say "and" out loud as you clap.
  • Now clap only on the "and" of 3 – a crucial upbeat in bebop phrasing (MC 16, 17).

Step 3 – Specific rhythm cells (3 minutes):

Clap these rhythm patterns from the Master Classes along with the recording:

  • Three eighth notes starting on the "and" of 3 (MC 16): (rest) (rest) and-of-3 4 and-of-4
  • Phrase using the "ands" of 4, 1, and 2 (MC 17): and-of-4 1 and-of-2 (rest)
  • ♭9 phrase rhythm (MC 17): clap the rhythm of a line that accents a ♭9 on an upbeat.

Step 4 – Scat the rhythms (2 minutes):

  • Scat a simple rhythmic phrase over the recording. No pitches, just rhythm. Make it swing.

Pocket Switching Drill (5 minutes)

Use the same recording or a blues play-along.

  1. Regular pocket (2 minutes): Snap or tap on beats 2 and 4. Play or scat a simple melody locked into this feel.
  2. Deep pocket – "the slow underneath" (2 minutes): Tap your foot at half the tempo (beats 1 and 3 of a slow 4/4, or just the 1 of every other bar). Keep playing the same melody at the original tempo. This is disorienting at first — it forces independence.
  3. Switch without stopping (1 minute): Play two choruses in regular pocket, then two choruses in deep pocket, without breaking the flow of the line. (From MC 10, 11.)
Pro tip: Bob Mover says the deepest pocket is called the "bottom pocket" — half of the deep pocket. Try tapping only on beat 1 of every four bars while playing at full tempo. (MC 10.)

60–80Tune of the Week: The 7 Steps MC 3, 13, 14, 15, 20, 21, 24, 26, 29

You will learn one standard deeply every week. Use the Seven Basic Steps to Be Your Own Real Book (MC 13, 14).

The 7 Steps

Work through these sequentially across the week. Each day, pick up where you left off.

DayStepActivity
Monday1. ListenListen to 3–5 definitive recordings. Do not play. Just absorb.
Tuesday2. Sing melody with lyricsFind the original sheet music. Learn the lyrics. Sing the melody with words along with a recording. (MC 24, 31, 32.)
Wednesday3. Identify points of restFind every place the melody and harmony naturally pause. Mark them on a lead sheet. (MC 1, 13.)
Thursday4. Map the ii–V–I cadencesTrace every ii–V–I leading to a point of rest. Play the roots of these cadences. (MC 14, 15.)
Friday5. Analyse with House of HarmonyDraw the House of Harmony for the tune's key. Identify tritone substitutions and parallel key relationships. (MC 16, 17, 18, 20, 26.)
Saturday6. Practise in 4-bar chunksTake one 4-bar phrase. Sing roots and melody. Play it on your instrument. Only move to the next chunk when this one is memorised. (MC 13, 15.)
Sunday7. Sing and play root movementPlay the bass line (roots and fifths) while singing the melody. Then reverse: play the melody and sing the bass line. (MC 3, 14.)

80–100Improvisation: Free Play with One New Concept MC 1, 9, 11, 22, 23, 30, 32, 33

This is where everything comes together. You will improvise on the tune of the week, but with a deliberate restriction: incorporate only one new concept per session. This prevents overwhelm and ensures deep internalisation.

The Rule

Play three choruses on the tune. In the first chorus, play freely. In the second and third choruses, consciously use the concept of the day.

Weekly Concept Rotation

DayConceptHow to Apply
MondayArpeggiation of chords only (MC 1)No scales. Build every phrase from chord tones (1–3–5–7–9). Sing the arpeggio first.
TuesdayVoice-leading lines (MC 20, 22, 36)Start each phrase on the 7th of one chord, resolve to the 3rd of the next. Use the four lines from your singing warm-up.
WednesdayChromatic passageways (MC 2)Insert a chromatic run connecting two target notes. Make it rhythmically intentional.
ThursdayHarmonic shift: ii Dorian → ♭II7 Mixolydian (MC 7, 32)On every ii–V, substitute the V7 with its tritone equivalent and play the diminished arpeggio.
FridayTargeting "wrong" notes (MC 22, 23)Deliberately land on a "wrong" note (♭9, ♯9, ♭13) on a strong beat, then resolve it by half-step. Own the tension.
SaturdayRiffing and pecking (MC 9, 30)Create short, rhythmic motifs. Repeat and develop them. Play from rhythm first, then assign pitches.
SundaySonny Stitt concept: melody & obligato (MC 33)Play one phrase of the tune's melody, leave space, then answer with an improvised comment. Call and response with yourself.

The Process

  • Minutes 80–85: Review the tune's melody and chord progression. Play one chorus of melody with backing track or metronome.
  • Minutes 85–95: Three choruses of improvisation with the daily concept. Record yourself.
  • Minutes 95–100: Listen back to your recording. Pick one phrase that worked and one that didn't. Analyse why.

100–110Mental Practice: Away from the Instrument MC 5, 34

Stan Getz said, "Learn to practise in your head." Adolph Sandole said, "Playing by ear is really playing by mind." (MC 34.)

This segment builds the internal instrument — the one that never needs reeds, valves, or tuning.

The Exercises (10 minutes)

Minute 100–103: Visualise the Keyboard or Fingerboard

  • Close your eyes. Picture your instrument's keyboard, fingerboard, or valve combinations.
  • Visualise playing a ii–V–I in a key you find difficult. See and feel every finger movement.
  • "Think of it like having a chessboard on the ceiling, or better: inside your mind." (MC 34.)

Minute 103–106: Helen Jordan's Exercise (MC 5)

  • Away from the instrument, sing and mentally visualise a ii–V–I in every key.
  • Start in C, move to F, B♭, E♭, A♭, D♭, G♭, B, E, A, D, G.
  • Hear the voice leading. Feel the fingerings in your mind.

Minute 106–110: A Cappella Solo

  • Choose the tune of the week. Sing a full chorus of improvisation a cappella, hearing the chord changes.
  • Scat with commitment. Use dynamics, articulation, and space.
  • Record yourself on your phone. Listen back. Did the lines make harmonic sense? Did they swing?

110–120Listening: Absorb the Masters MC 18, 19, 21, 26, 27, 31, 32, 34

This is not background music. This is active, immersive listening. You are downloading phrasing, time, sound, and spirit directly from the source.

The Listening List

Choose one recording per day. Sit still. Do nothing else.

The Process (10 minutes)

  • Minute 110–115: Listen with your eyes closed. Tap the pocket (2 and 4 or deep pocket).
  • Minute 115–118: Listen again. This time, sing along with the solo or melody.
  • Minute 118–120: Turn off the recording. Sit in silence for two minutes. Let the music echo in your mind.

Summary Checklist

Before you close your practice session, ask yourself:

  • Did I sing every exercise before I played it?
  • Did I use a metronome on 2 and 4 for at least one segment?
  • Did I work on one tune deeply, not five tunes shallowly?
  • Did I record myself and listen back critically?
  • Did I spend time away from the instrument, practising mentally?
  • Did I listen to a master recording with full attention?
  • Did I end with free, joyful playing — not just drills?
"Jazz is a language. To speak it, you must first hear it in your head and feel it in your body. The instrument is just the amplifier. Sing, tap, listen, and then — only then — play." — Bob Mover's teaching philosophy
