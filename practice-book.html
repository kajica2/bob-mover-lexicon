The Complete JazzAbility Practice Book

A self-guided mastery system based on Bob Mover's Jazz Master Classes.

Introduction: How to Use This Practice Book

This book distills over 40 Master Classes and foundational courses into a single, logical practice path. The material is drawn directly from Bob Mover's JazzAbility curriculum – a lifetime of wisdom from a saxophonist who played with Charles Mingus, Chet Baker, and countless others.

The core philosophy:

  • Sing everything first – your ear is the ultimate judge.
  • Understand numerically – think in scale degrees and voice-leading, not just note names.
  • Feel the time deep in your body – rhythm is not an afterthought, it's the ground you walk on.
  • Learn tunes from the inside out – melody, harmony, and lyrics together.

Work through the book in order. Each section contains exercises, listening assignments, and practice routines. At the end, you'll find master class summaries that connect every concept to a specific session so you can dive even deeper into the video lessons.

Part I – Foundations

What We Do & How to Do It (1st Steps & Next Steps)

Source courses: Playing Jazz: 1st Steps, Next Steps, Harmony, Melody & More

1. The Elements of Improvisation

Every jazz line is built from a small set of options:

  • Arpeggiation of chords (1–3–5–7–9–11–13)
  • Scale tones & permutations (diatonic and chromatic)
  • Neighbor notes & enclosures (chromatic approaches)
  • Passing tones (connecting chord tones)
  • Licks (learned phrases, but never as crutches)

Practice: Pick a II-V-I in one key. Limit yourself to one option at a time.

  • Day 1: only chord tones (arpeggios).
  • Day 2: add scale-wise movement between them.
  • Day 3: insert one chromatic passing tone between every chord tone.

2. Scales & Permutations – The Building Blocks

Don't just run scales up and down. Permute them to break the finger-habit and open your ears.

Example in C major:

  • Groups of 3: C-D-E, D-E-F, E-F-G…
  • Groups of 4: C-D-E-F, D-E-F-G…
  • Alternating direction: up a 3rd, down a step, up a 4th, down a 3rd…
  • Chromatic inserts: 2, ♯2, 3, 4, ♯4, 5 and then back down (as in MC 35).

Sing first, then play. Use numerical solfeggio: 1, 2, ♯2, 3, 4, ♯4, 5 (major scale).

3. The Idea of Practicing Licks

A lick is a musical sentence you've internalised. The goal is not to copy-paste, but to absorb vocabulary that you can reconstruct, permute, and place in new contexts.

Method (from MC 35):

  • Learn a phrase by ear, phrase-by-phrase, singing it until it's inside you.
  • Analyse the note functions (9th of V7, ♭13, resolution to 3rd of I, etc.).
  • Take the phrase through symmetrical intervalic combinations – play it starting on every scale degree, then move it in major 3rds, minor 3rds, tritones.

Part II – Ear Training & Singing

The Heart of the JazzAbility Method

Source courses: Master Classes 4, 12, 13, 15, 17, 21, 22, 36–40

Every Master Class begins with singing. This is non-negotiable. "Singing is key to ear training" (MC 4).

1. Daily Singing Drills

Sing the following, using numerical solfeggio and traditional solfeggio, in all twelve keys:

  • Major scale
  • Harmonic minor (from the root and from the 5th)
  • Melodic minor (ascending form)
  • Diminished scale (whole-half)
  • Whole-tone scale

Then sing intervals and short patterns:

5 → ♯5 → 6 → 7 → 1

1 → 7 → 6 → ♭6 → 5

5 → 6 → ♭7 → 7 → 1

1 → ♭7 → 6 → ♭6 → 5  (the "Ellington" line)

Sing these over a drone or a play-along.

2. Sing the Great Solos

Choose one solo at a time. The curriculum repeatedly returns to:

  • Lester Young – "Lady Be Good"
  • Charlie Parker – "Yardbird Suite", "Out of Nowhere"
  • Miles Davis – "Solar"
  • Chet Baker – "There Will Never Be Another You"

How to practise:

  1. Sing the melody with the recording and with lyrics if available.
  2. Tap your foot, snap on 2 and 4, then try the deep pocket (half-time feel).
  3. Sing the solos along with the record, phrase by phrase.
  4. Sing a cappella, hearing the changes in your head.

3. Voice Leading Lines – The Glue of Harmony

Learn to sing and play voice leading lines through II-V-I progressions. (From MC 20-23, 36-39)

Core patterns (sing in numbers):

  • 9th of V7 (which is 5th of II-7) → ♯9 of V7 → Major 7th of I
  • 7th of II-7 (sus4 of V7) → ♭9 of V7 → 5th or Lydian ♯4 of I
  • 7th of II-7 → ♯11 of V7 → 9th of I
  • 13th of V7 (9th of II-7) → ♭13th of V7 → 9th of I

Sing these lines over a II-V-I vamp. Then play them on your instrument, in every key.

Part III – Harmony & Melody

The Architecture of a Jazz Line

Source courses: Harmony, Melody & More; MC 1, 16-18, 20, 26, 29, 33, 40

1. Guide Tones – The Skeleton

The 3rds and 7ths of every chord are butter notes (MC 4). They define the harmony.

Exercise:

  • Take the tune "Solar" (or any standard).
  • Play only the 3rd and 7th of each chord in half notes.
  • Make it swing – give the line a rhythm.

2. The House of Harmony & Substitutions

This is a systematic way of seeing chords by their function, not just their name. (Introduced MC 16, deepened 17-20.)

The duplex house:

For any major key, build an identical "house" a tritone away. The two houses share walls – chords can slide from one room to the other.

  • In C, the parallel key is G♭ (the ♭5 key).
  • The II-V-I in C (Dm7-G7-Cmaj7) can be substituted with A♭m7-D♭7-Cmaj7 (the ♭5 sub).

Practice:

  • Draw the "House of Harmony" for a tune ("Cherokee" is used extensively in MC 18-20).
  • Identify every II-V-I and its possible tritone substitutions.
  • Improvise using lines that "bounce" between the two houses.

3. Paraphrasing & Melodic Reconstruction

Paraphrasing is the art of ornamenting a melody while keeping its identity. (MC 24-27, 31-33)

The 12-Step Levels (MC 24):

  1. Straight melody, sheet-music style.
  2. Straight melody but syncopated (big-band feel).
  3. The "Melody Game" – change one rhythm, keep pitches; then vice versa.
  4. Add a single passing tone.
  5. Add enclosures around the melody notes.
  6. Combine rhythmic alteration with enclosures.
  7. Use line clichés on cadences (MC 24, 30).
  8. Fragment the melody – play one phrase, leave space, insert a comment (Sonny Stitt concept, MC 33).
  9. Scat an improvised chorus on the tune's changes (MC 30).
  10. Play the melody in a different metre.
  11. Play a contrafact – use the chords but invent a new melody.
  12. Full reconstruction – the listener barely recognises the original until the bridge.

Apply this to "There Will Never Be Another You", "Out of Nowhere", and "I'll Remember April".

4. Harmonic Options on II-V-I (The Advanced Colours)

From MC 8, 9, 14, 32:

  • Dorian on the II- chord (standard).
  • Harmonic minor a half-step above the V7 chord (e.g., A♭ harmonic minor over G7) – gives ♭9, ♯9, ♭13.
  • Melodic minor up a 5th from the V7 (D melodic minor over G7) – gives ♯11.
  • Dorian of the key a minor 3rd above – for C major, use E dorian over Dm7, giving an F♯ colour.
  • Harmonic Shift: from II-7 Dorian to ♭II7 Mixolydian (the b5 substitute).

Exercise: On a II-V-I loop, play one chorus using only one of the above options. The next chorus, use a different option.

Part IV – Rhythm & Time

The Pocket Is Everything

Source courses: MC 5, 9-12, 16-19, 22, 23, 30

1. Meet the Beats

You must know exactly where every beat and off-beat is.

Clapping/Counting drill:

  • Put on "Hot House" by Charlie Parker / Dizzy Gillespie.
  • Clap on "and of 4, and of 1, and of 2" (a recurring phrase in MC 17).
  • Then clap three eighth notes starting on the "and of 3".

2. The Three Pockets (MC 5, 10, 11)

  • Regular pocket – tapping on 2 and 4.
  • Deep pocket (long meter) – half-time feel; the slow underneath.
  • Bottom pocket – half of the deep pocket; a glacial, floating time sense.

Practice: Play (or sing) the melody of a ballad while physically tapping the deep pocket with your foot. Then switch to the regular pocket without losing the feel.

3. African 6 Against 4/4 (MC 12)

Quarter-note triplets and eighth-note triplets in various combinations against a 4/4 swing feel.

Exercise:

  • Set metronome on half note = 60 (beats 2 and 4).
  • Play continuous eighth-note triplets.
  • Then alternates: two bars of eighth-notes, two bars of quarter-note triplets.

4. Playing from a Rhythmic Concept (MC 9)

Before you think about notes, think about rhythm.

  • Scat a rhythmic phrase over the form of a tune.
  • Then assign pitches.
  • Use "riffing" and "pecking" – short, percussive melodic cells.

Part V – Learning Tunes

How to Be Your Own Real Book

Source courses: MC 3, 7, 13-15, 20, 21, 24, 26, 29, 38-40

The Seven Basic Steps (MC 13, 14)

  1. Listen to multiple definitive recordings of the tune.
  2. Sing the melody with lyrics (find the original sheet music).
  3. Identify the points of rest – where the melody and harmony naturally pause.
  4. Map the II-V-I cadences that lead to those points of rest.
  5. Analyse the tune using the House of Harmony.
  6. Practise in 4-bar chunks, incrementally – never move on until the chunk is internalised.
  7. Sing and play the root movement (bass line) while singing the melody.

Tune Studies (Included in the Curriculum)

  • Solar (Miles Davis) – harmonic shifts, voice leading, diminished arpeggios (MC 6-8, 11).
  • Cherokee – points of rest, back-door dominants, bouncing ball through the house (MC 14, 15, 18, 20).
  • What Is This Thing Called Love – harmonic minor to major, melodic minor on V7, Dorian of relative major (MC 8-10, 14).
  • There Will Never Be Another You – line clichés, 12-step paraphrase (MC 24, 25, 30-32).
  • Out of Nowhere – contrafacts, a cappella singing, listening to Bird and Ella (MC 25-27, 29).
  • I'll Remember April – lyrics, scatting, great solos (MC 38-40).

For each tune, go through the seven steps before you attempt to improvise.

Part VI – Improvisation Tools & Vocabulary

Source courses: MC 1, 2, 7, 11, 16, 20, 22, 23, 35

1. Chromatic Passageways (MC 2)

A string of chromatic notes that connect diatonic targets. Example over Rhythm changes: descending chromatic line from the 5th to the 3rd of the tonic.

Practice: Take a II-V-I and connect the 3rd of the II- chord to the 7th of the I chord chromatically.

2. Sequences (MC 7, 11, 16)

A melodic motif repeated at different pitch levels, often following the chord changes.

Diminished arpeggio sequence through "Solar" (MC 7): Play a diminished arpeggio up from the 3rd of each II-7 chord, shifting harmonically.

3. Targeting & Making "Wrong" Notes Sound Right (MC 22, 23)

  • Decide on a target note for the end of the phrase (usually a chord tone on a strong beat).
  • Approach it from a half-step above or below, or with an enclosure.
  • If you land on a "wrong" note, do not panic – resolve it by step to a chord tone, or repeat it and treat it as a passing tone. The ear accepts what you accept.
  • Level 2 (MC 23): Play a completely chromatic line that still resolves to a target. The logic of the resolution makes the preceding dissonance sound intentional.

4. Riffing & Scatting (MC 30)

  • Riff on the blues: create a 2-bar call, 2-bar response. Sing it, then play it.
  • Scat an eighth-note-oriented chorus over a standard. This forces you to create melodic lines without finger patterns.

5. Learning Bebop Phrases in All Keys (MC 35)

  1. Learn the phrase phrase-by-phrase by ear.
  2. Analyse: "That's a ♭9 on V7 moving to the 5th of I".
  3. Play the phrase in the original key.
  4. Move it through symmetrical intervalic combinations: up in whole steps, minor 3rds, major 3rds, tritones.

Part VII – Practice Techniques & Mental Training

Source courses: MC 2, 5, 13, 21, 34

The 8 Ways to Practice Just About Everything (MC 2)

Take any single element (e.g., major 2nds, a lick, a voice-leading line) and:

  1. Play it slowly, with a metronome on every beat.
  2. Play it with a metronome on 2 and 4.
  3. Play it in a different octave.
  4. Sing it, then play it.
  5. Play it starting on a different beat of the bar.
  6. Transpose it to a different key (by interval, not by rote).
  7. Invert it (up a third becomes down a third).
  8. Insert it into a tune you're working on.

How to Practice Without Your Instrument (MC 5)

Stan Getz said, "Learn to practice in your head."

  • Helen Jordan's exercise: Visualise and sing V-VII-I or II-V-I in every key while walking down the street.
  • Mental play-along: Hear the rhythm section and sing your solo a cappella.
  • Visualisation: "Think of it like having a chessboard on the ceiling, or better: inside your mind" (MC 34).

Playing While Practicing (MC 4, 5)

Always balance drill with music.

  • End every practice session by playing a tune freely, incorporating the new material.
  • Sculpt the phrase – dynamics, vibrato, articulation.

Incremental Chunking (MC 13, 15)

Break tunes into 4-bar phrases. Work on one chunk until you can:

  • Sing the melody and roots.
  • Play the phrase in three different positions/keys.
  • Connect it smoothly to the next chunk.

Appendices

A. Daily Practice Routine (120 minutes)

See the full Daily Practice Routine page.

  • 10 min – Singing: scales, intervals, voice-leading lines.
  • 15 min – Technique: a permutation or arpeggio exercise, in all keys using the 8-way method.
  • 20 min – Ear training: sing a solo (Parker, Young, Davis) with recording, then play it.
  • 15 min – Rhythm: meet-the-beats drill, pocket switching with a recording.
  • 20 min – Tune of the week: work through the 7 steps on one standard.
  • 20 min – Improvisation: free play on the tune, incorporating one new concept (e.g., harmonic shift, line cliché, targeting).
  • 10 min – Mental practice: away from instrument, visualise a tune and sing a solo a cappella.
  • 10 min – Listening: put on a master and just absorb the time, sound, and phrasing.
